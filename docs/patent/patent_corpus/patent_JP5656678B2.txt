A distinct canine pedigree is characterized by a unique collection of morphology, behavior, and disease prevalence (2). Various dog morphologies have existed since 1000 years ago, and reproductive isolation between dogs was formalized with the advent of pedigree clubs and pedigree standards in the mid-19th century. Since that time, the “breed barrier” rule (ie, the rule that if both a dog's female parent and male parent are not registered members, no dog can be a registered member) Has been issued, guaranteeing a relatively closed genetic pool between each breed. ３５０種類以上の遺伝性障害は、純血種イヌ個体群を分離する（非特許文献３）。これらの多くはヒトの一般的な障害と類似しており、特有の形態を生成するために使用された積極的な同系交配プログラムの結果として、特定の品種または品種群に限定されている。 More than 350 hereditary disorders separate purebred dog populations (Non-Patent Document 3). Because historical records vary from breed to breed in reliability, genetic analysis that is not based on previous population information is the most direct and accurate method of determining population structure. Over the past decade, molecular methods have been used to deepen our understanding of wild canine species and to determine the relationship between domestic dogs and those wild species. Mitochondrial DNA sequence analysis explains the relationship between domestic dogs and wolves and elucidates multiple domestication events that occurred 40,000 to 100,000 years ago (Non-Patent Document 4; Non-Patent Document 5; Non-Patent Documents) 6). However, the evolution of mitochondrial DNA is too slow to make it possible to infer relationships between modern breeds. Most modern breeds have existed for less than 400 years. In addition, the phylogenetic distance scale and phylogenetic tree creation program have no function to handle reticulated evolution as commonly observed in dog populations (Non-Patent Document 7; Non-Patent Document 8; Non-Patent Document 9) ). One previous study showed that nuclear microsatellite loci can be used to assign dogs from five species to their source species and show large genetic distances between these breeds (10). ). Another study used microsatellite to detect the association of two pairs in 28 collections, but failed to demonstrate a broad phylogenetic relationship between varieties (non-patent literature). 9). Failure to find such an association may reflect the nature of the microsatellite locus (Non-Patent Document 9), the limited number of varieties examined, or the analytical methods used in this study. Instead, this failure may reflect a complex structure in purebred dog populations due to the closest origin of most breeds and a mix of ancestral types in their organisms. Cluster analysis) Cluster analysis was performed using the multilocus genotype clustering program structure (Pritchard et al. (2000) Genetics 155: 945-59; Falush et al. (2003) Science 299: 1582-5). The program utilizes a Bayesian model-based clustering algorithm to identify genetically distinct subpopulations based on allelic frequency patterns. Multiple runs were completed for each K value (number of genetic clusters) with a burn-in length of 10,000 steps and a Gibbs sampler set to repeat 100,000 times.
